# 4. Magnetic Torque

**The Simple Explanation:**
We have a wire shaped like a rectangle sitting in a magnetic field. Because electricity is running through it, the magnetic field is going to try and flip it over. We need to calculate how strong that flipping force (torque) is.

**The Setup:**
* Area of the loop ($A$) = $10 \text{ cm} \times 5 \text{ cm} = 0.1 \text{ m} \times 0.05 \text{ m} = 0.005 \text{ m}^2$
* Current ($I$) = $2 \text{ A}$
* Magnetic Field ($B$) = $0.3 \text{ T}$
* Angle ($\theta$) = The field is parallel to the loop, meaning the angle to the *normal* (straight up from the loop) is $90^\circ$. $\sin(90^\circ) = 1$.

**Step-by-Step Math:**
The formula for magnetic torque ($\tau$) is Current $\times$ Area $\times$ Magnetic Field:

$$
\tau = I \cdot A \cdot B \cdot \sin(\theta)
$$

Plug in the numbers:

$$
\tau = 2 \cdot 0.005 \cdot 0.3 \cdot 1
$$

**Final Answer:**

$$
\tau = 0.003 \text{ N m}
$$
